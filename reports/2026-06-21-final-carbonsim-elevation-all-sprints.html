Section 2

Executive Summary

The roadmap is done. CarbonSim Online began this effort as a functional but shallow compliance simulator — static offset prices, three single-year bot heuristics, one-shot event cards, and an XP bar wired to nothing. Five sprints later it is a tight strategy game with the mechanical depth the brief targeted: prices that respond to demand, AI competitors that plan multiple years ahead and trade with the player, an event deck that remembers its own consequences, a regulatory-climate meter that bends the rules, and a persistent unlock tree that carries progress between sessions across three real-world carbon markets.

Crucially, every layer was added on top of the existing pure-reducer architecture — never a rewrite. The research brief's central warning (a static offset price creates a dominant strategy) was answered in Sprint 2 and then defended at every subsequent sprint with a Monte-Carlo dominance sweep. Where new mechanics threatened balance — twice, materially — the regressions were caught by that sweep and corrected before merge.

Recommendation
Ship it. The five-sprint core is feature-complete, tested, and balanced. The highest-value next investments are not new systems but connecting the ones now in place: hard-gate the unlock-tree nodes to the mechanics they name, teach the AI to use financing, and add the end-game star rating (DEC-002) that ties XP, survival, and penalties into a single win screen.
Section 3

Background & Objective

CarbonSim Online is a FastAPI + vanilla-JS carbon-compliance management game with a canvas isometric city. The player runs a Vietnamese industrial company through a multi-year emissions-trading scheme, balancing abatement, offsets, auctions, and trades against a declining cap.

Objective: "Elevate CarbonSim Online to Kairosoft/Sid Meier/tycoon sophistication while deeply reflecting carbon-market domain mechanics." The architecture was already sound — an OpenRCT2-style deterministic reducer with an immutable audit log and snapshot system. Research confirmed this is the right architecture at ~8-company scale and must not be replaced. Every sprint therefore layered mechanics onto the reducer, sequenced by leverage-per-effort: determinism first, then market feedback, AI, story, and meta-progression last.

Section 6

Methodology

  • Reducer-first, additive layering. Every sprint added state and serviced it through the existing apply_company_decision / _start_year / _close_current_year hooks. No mechanic ever became the new emissions source of truth — even capex rides alongside the original active_abatement_ids.
  • Balance as a gate, not an afterthought. From Sprint 3 onward, a seeded Monte-Carlo run_strategy_sweep ran every sprint. The rule: no single strategy may exceed a 60% win rate. This caught two real regressions and drove every balance re-tune.
  • Determinism as a regression guard. A same-seed determinism check ran each sprint; it also surfaced a latent shared-state bug in Sprint 3.
  • Migration guards for forward-compatibility. Each new state field is setdefault-guarded in _start_year, so saves from earlier sprints load without a migration step.
  • Per-sprint ceremony. Each sprint ended with implementation → commit/push → a phase report; this document is the capstone synthesis.

Section 7

Phase-by-Phase Analysis

SPRINT 1
Foundation — Determinism, Consequence Preview, Year-End Ceremony 65eafb9

Seeded RNG through state for true replay, a pure project_outcome so every decision can show its compliance/cash impact before commit, and a Kairosoft-style year-end report. This was the enabling layer: without the seed, later balance sweeps would be meaningless; without project_outcome, neither the UI previews nor the Sprint 3 AI could reason about consequences.

SPRINT 2
Market — Dynamic Pricing + Full Instrument Stack c6a9a6f

Replaced the static offset price with a BazaarBot demand-response model (P = P₀(1 + ε·min(d/S,1)), ε=0.4) and surfaced forwards and VCM project investment. The negative feedback loop the research prescribed: buying offsets raises their price within the year, killing the buy-everything dominant strategy. Playtest penalty variation came in at a 5.1% coefficient of variation — well inside tolerance.

SPRINT 3
AI — Goal-Driven Agents + Dominance Sweep ec6b29d

Replaced single-year bot heuristics with CompanyAgents that plan over a 2–3 year horizon across the full instrument stack and propose OTC trades. The new run_strategy_sweep immediately earned its keep: it revealed that at the inherited penalty rate, simply ignoring compliance was the optimal play — which would have hollowed out the carbon theme. Raising the penalty so compliance is rational flattened the field to a 0.45 max win rate. The sweep also exposed a latent shared-state bug (the cap schedule aliased a global dict and was mutated in place).

SPRINT 4
Story — Cascading Cards + Policy Stability + City Canvas da4b1ce

A policy_stability meter (0–100) bends the cap and fires crackdowns/relief; cards gained requires_condition, sets_condition, follow_on_cards, and multi-year effect_duration, turning isolated events into arcs. The feedback loop initially created a death-spiral that re-broke balance; mean-reverting the meter and removing a penalty-rate ratchet restored it. The city canvas now shows the regulatory climate as a visible haze.

SPRINT 5
Meta — Capex Abatement + XP Unlock Tree + Jurisdiction Skins 1493c6c

Abatement became a capex asset financeable by loan, with annual opex and seeded tech-failure risk; an XP engine feeds a 7-node unlock tree persisted to a player_profiles table; and the whole sim re-skins to EU ETS or California. The recurring lesson held one last time — each jurisdiction's distinct offset economics moved the balance point, so each had its penalty re-tuned until no strategy passed 60%.

Key findings

  • The architecture held. Five layers of mechanics, zero rewrites. The reducer + audit log + snapshot pattern absorbed everything, validating the research's "do not change it" verdict.
  • Balance is the load-bearing discipline. Three separate penalty re-tunes (Sprint 3, and per-jurisdiction in Sprint 5) plus the Sprint 4 meter redesign all traced back to one truth: a mechanic is only fun if no single response dominates. The sweep made that measurable.
  • Additive beats invasive. Capex/loans/tech-risk shipped without touching the emissions math by riding alongside it — preserving four sprints of tuning.

Tradeoffs & rejected options

  • Cap modulation over full formula replacement (Sprint 4) — kept calibrated decline curves intact.
  • Demand-accumulator over a full BazaarBot clearing market (ALT-001) — same feedback loop, a fraction of the complexity.
  • Template year-end text over LLM generation (ALT-002) — deterministic and offline.
  • ECS refactor rejected outright (ALT-003) — wrong scale at ~8 companies.
