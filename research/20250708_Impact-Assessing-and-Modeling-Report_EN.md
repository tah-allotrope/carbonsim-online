EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Emission Trading System (ETS) is a critical and cost-efficient policy instrument for countries to
achieve their greenhouse gas (GHG) reduction targets and fulfil international commitments such
as the Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) and Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP).
Besides climate change benefits, ETS can also have broad socio-economic and environmental
implications for businesses, industries, and the overall economy, which requires careful
consideration before commencing the operation of the system.

According to the 2020 Law on Environmental Protection and its associated regulations, Viet Nam
plans to pilot the ETS in 2025, with full implementation scheduled for 2029. While certain design
and governance aspects of the ETS have been considered under the draft amended Decree No.
06/2022/ND-CP on GHG emission reductions and ozone layer protection, a comprehensive
assessment of the impacts of different ETS governance options has not yet been conducted.

This study is part of the Technical Assistance "Development and Impacts Assessment of Carbon
Credit and Allowance Governance Mechanisms in Viet Nam", implemented by the Southeast Asia
Energy Transition Partnership (ETP) to support the Department of Climate Change (DCC), Ministry
of Agriculture and Environment (MAE) on carbon market development in Viet Nam. The main
objective of the study is to assess and model the socio-economic and environmental impacts of
various ETS governance options in Viet Nam in order to provide evidence-based insights and
enhance the capacity of policymakers to develop the legal framework to operationalise the carbon
market in Viet Nam.

This report evaluates ETS governance options using econometric models, focusing on the socio-
economic impacts in the pilot implementation period. The study applies a three-level approach:
enterprise, industry, and macroeconomy. At the enterprise level, the report assesses the GHG
emissions of facilities covered under Decision 13/2024/QD-TTg with a focus on thermal power,
steel, and cement production facilities. At the industry level, the study calculates investment costs
to meet the NDC emission reduction targets under the scenarios without the ETS and analyses
sectoral impacts with the ETS interventions. The various NDC scenarios covered in the assessment
include those that aligned with Viet Nam's unconditional, conditional commitments, and the
conditional NDC target with JETP. At the macroeconomic level, the econometric model evaluates
the impact of ETS on key macroeconomic indicators such as GDP, investment, consumption, and
consumer prices. The findings from the assessment across the three levels offer insights into the
effects on enterprises, sectors targeted for the ETS pilot, and the broader economy, ultimately
providing feasible policy recommendations.

The study highlights the vital role of the ETS in enabling Viet Nam to achieve its emission reduction
targets cost-effectively, while facilitating the transition to a sustainable economic model. The
analysis focuses on three key industries: thermal power, steel, and cement. The Consultant
developed nine policy scenarios to evaluate both economic and environmental impacts, laying a
solid foundation for proposing an effective governance framework.

The study results show that during the pilot phase, the carbon price fluctuates from 1.1 to 3.7
USD/tCO, achieving the most optimal cost when supply and demand approach equilibrium. The
carbon credit price tends to decrease as supply increases. Expanding the offset limit to 20% may

further lower the carbon price, and it would be the lowest price under the unconditional NDC
scenario since there is a big gap between demand and supply. On the other hand, the ETS
significantly reduces compliance costs for businesses, especially in conditional scenarios. Under
the unconditional NDC scenario, compliance costs decrease from 420 million USD to 164.2 million
USD with the implementation of the ETS, while in the conditional NDC scenario, costs drop from

## billion USD to 927.6 million USD.

By industry, thermal power benefits the most from the ETS, experiencing a significant reduction in
compliance costs. The cement industry can enhance profitability by selling surplus allowances
resulting from the implementation of NDC emission reduction measures, while the steel industry
is less affected, though it shows variability across different scenarios. ETS further enables
businesses to optimise compliance costs and generate additional revenue from allowance
transactions, with thermal power emerging as the main buyer and cement as the primary supplier
of allowances on the market. At the macroeconomic level, ETS helps mitigate the negative impacts
of emission reduction policies on GDP, limiting growth declines through measures such as
expanding the offset limit and leveraging international support mechanisms such as JETP.

In addition to reducing emissions, ETS acts as a driving force for economic restructuring. By
incentivising businesses to adopt GHG reduction measures, the system pressures high-emission
industries to transition to cleaner technologies, while simultaneously creating strong growth
opportunities in renewable energy and electrified transport sectors. Meanwhile, a portion of
financial resources is reallocated from high-emission sectors to areas that generate carbon credits
and to sectors capable of producing carbon allowances, such as the cement industry in this case.
Thus, the ETS lays a critical foundation for the green transition in Viet Nam's economy.

Finally, the choice of an NDC-based governance option has direct impacts on both emission
reduction costs and broader economic effects. Without the application of ETS, the achievement of
the emission reduction target would be more expensive in all scenarios. While the 10% carbon
credit offset (ETS10) helps reduce costs, the 20% offset (ETS20) provides the optimal result,
minimising impacts on GDP and enhancing market liquidity and flexibility. Ultimately, ETS not only
helps Viet Nam reduce emissions at the lowest cost but also promotes green growth, enhances
business competitiveness, and ensures sustainable long-term development.

The study recommends the implementation of the ETS in Viet Nam to allow flexibility in achieving
NDC emission reduction targets, while optimising costs and minimising negative impacts on the
economy and society. Among the evaluated options, ETS20 in the unconditional NDC scenario is
the most favourable, due to its low compliance costs, minimal impacts on GDP, consumption, and
prices, and the potential for generating revenue from the carbon market. Notably, ETS20 stands
out for its potential to increase revenue without substantially heightening macroeconomic risks.

In both NDC conditional and NDC conditional-with-JETP scenarios, ETS20 continues to
demonstrate its superiority, yielding the highest market revenue and facilitating the mobilisation
of financial resources for NDC implementation. Furthermore, it enhances connectivity with other
sectors in the domestic carbon market and with the global carbon market through the potential
use of carbon credits for offsets. In contrast, non-ETS scenarios, while simple to operate, come
with high compliance costs, significant macroeconomic impacts, and a lack of market incentives.

In conclusion, a flexible ETS with offset limits of 10�20% (ETS10 � ETS20) emerges as the most
viable option for the initial phase of the carbon market in Viet Nam. Among these, ETS20 within
the unconditional NDC scenario represents the most feasible and effective pilot option, laying a
robust foundation for the expansion of the market and enhancement of Viet Nam's climate
commitments in the future.

I. INTRODUCTION

I.1. Background

The Emissions Trading System (ETS) is widely regarded as one of the most effective policy
instruments for reducing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. However, its impacts extend beyond
environmental control, profoundly influencing business behaviour and operational efficiency. A
critical challenge lies in assessing these impacts at the enterprise level, industry level, and across
the entire national economy to provide a comprehensive perspective for policymakers.

At the business level, the ETS implementation directly affects firms' operational and financial
strategies by introducing a price on carbon emissions. Companies with high emissions would
either invest in cleaner technologies to reduce emissions or purchase allowances and carbon
credits in the market to cover their emissions, thereby increasing compliance costs. This
incentivises innovation in energy efficiency and low-carbon technologies. On the other hand, firms
that successfully reduce emissions below their allocated allowances can sell surplus permits,
creating an economic incentive for proactive mitigation measures. However, the ETS can also
impose financial burdens on companies with limited access to capital for green investments,
particularly small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). The extent of these impacts depends on
the scope and coverage of the ETS, the overall stringency of the cap, and the selected allowance
allocation methods.

At the industry level, the ETS influences competitiveness, market structure, and investment
patterns. Industries with high emissions intensity face increased production costs, which may lead
to shifts in market dynamics as firms seek cost-effective ways to reduce emissions. In some cases,
this may accelerate the adoption of cleaner technologies or the transition to alternative business
models. However, industries exposed to international competition may experience carbon
leakage, where production relocates to countries with laxer carbon regulations. To mitigate this,
policymakers may implement measures such as free allocation of allowances or carbon border
adjustment mechanisms (CBAM). Overall, the ETS fosters a transition toward a more sustainable
industrial sector by steering capital flows toward low-carbon investments, although the level of
impact depends on governance design and operation of the market.

At the macro-economic level, ETS influences resource reallocation, steering the economy toward
a more sustainable trajectory. While transition costs may exert short-term pressure on Gross
Domestic Product (GDP), Investment, and consumption, these effects are often mitigated by the
long-term economic opportunities, driven by technological advancements, efficiency gains, and
product transformation. However, the introduction of ETS may lead to rising energy prices, which
could disproportionately affect low-income groups and energy-intensive industries. A well-
balanced governance framework that upholds environmental objectives while ensuring economic
resilience requires a comprehensive understanding of the socio-economic and environmental
impacts of the ETS. A thorough assessment of these impacts is essential to identify potential
challenges and derive effective policy interventions to mitigate unintended consequences and
optimise the system's effectiveness.

I.2. Scope of work and objectives

The Technical Assistance "Development and Impacts Assessment of Carbon Credit and Allowance
Governance Mechanism in Viet Nam" has been launched under the Southeast Asia Energy
Transition Partnership (ETP) to support the Department of Climate Change (DCC) within Ministry
of Agriculture and Environment (MAE) in Viet Nam. Its primary objective is to conduct a
comprehensive analysis and model the impacts of different governance mechanisms for carbon
credits and GHG emission allowances. The evidence-based insights derived from this Technical
Assistance will inform the development of a national legal framework to facilitate the effective
operationalisation of Viet Nam's carbon market.

As part of this Technical Assistance, this study specifically corresponds to Deliverable 3: A report
on assessing and modelling the impacts of ETS governance options in Viet Nam. This deliverable
builds upon the findings on governance options for ETS in the pilot phase from Deliverable 2,
"Analysis of Viet Nam's legal framework and international experiences to identify governance
options for emission trading system in Viet Nam". The study conducts an assessment based on a
quantitative model to evaluate the environmental and socio-economic impacts of governance
options for the pilot implementation of the ETS in Viet Nam. It specifically examines the effects on
energy-intensive industries, with a focus on GHG emissions, energy transition, the national
economy, and Viet Nam's climate commitments under Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC)
and Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP) in order to provide a data-driven analysis and
quantitative insights to support the design of an optimal governance framework for the ETS in Viet
Nam.

I.3. Methodological approach

Evaluating the socio-economic and environmental impacts of an ETS requires a comprehensive
approach that integrates both qualitative and quantitative methods. Given the multifaceted nature
of ETS effects, ranging from corporate financial performance to broader macroeconomic shifts,
environmental outcomes, and social distributional implications, this study adopts a multi-level
methodology, starting at the enterprise level and extending to industry-wide and macroeconomic
analyses.

At the micro level, GHG emissions from facilities potentially covered in the ETS pilot phase are
identified. The list of facilities is based on Decision No. 13/2024/QD-TTg, dated 13 August 2024,
issued by the Prime Minister, promulgating the list of sectors, GHG emitting establishments
subject to GHG inventory (updated version) (Decision No. 13/2024/QD-TTg). The approach and
options for setting the emission cap and allocation of allowances are performed based on the
recommendations from Deliverable 2, "Analysis of Viet Nam's legal framework and international
experiences to identify governance options for emission trading system in Viet Nam". Data on their
financial and trade performance are also assessed to provide inputs for the sectoral analysis.

Sectors considered for ETS pilot implementation are those with high emissions, as they are
expected to play a crucial role in achieving NDC targets. Based on the recommendations from the
previous Deliverable and to be aligned with the roadmap for development of the carbon market
in Viet Nam defined at Decision No. 232/QD/TTg of the Prime Minister dated 24 January 2025, and
the Government's considerations in the draft amended Decree No. 06/2022/ND-CP published in
December 2024, the study analyses the impacts on thermal power, steel and cement production

industries. These are three sectors that have been demonstrated to be major sources of GHG
emissions, and international experience also indicates that establishing an ETS for these sectors
can generate significant mitigation potential, improve cost-effectiveness through market
mechanisms, and create a foundation for gradually expanding the ETS to other sectors. To
evaluate the impacts of the ETS on these sectors, the study employs a cost-benefit analysis to
estimate the financial implications of compliance, including abatement costs, operational changes,
and potential benefits from emissions trading based on publicly available information, including
Decree No. 06/2022/ND-CP, Decision No. 13/2024/QD-TTg, the NDC 2022, and other secondary
sources.

At the macro level, a structural econometric model is utilised to assess the broader economic
impact of ETS. This model integrates key economic indicators, such as GDP, investment dynamics,
consumption and employment effects.

The assessment at these levels is conducted under various NDC scenarios, including:

     Unconditional NDC target scenario: aligns with emission reductions that Viet Nam commits

          to achieving with domestic resources;
      Conditional NDC target scenario: aligns with additional emission reductions that depend

          on international support through finance, technology transfer, capacity building, and
          market-based mechanisms;
      Conditional NDC target with JETP: aligns with further reductions leveraged from enhanced
          financial and technical assistance under the JETP framework.

To comprehensively evaluate the effects of the ETS, the study assesses the socio-economic and
environmental impacts both with and without the implementation of ETS. This scenario-based
comparative approach helps to quantify and isolate the specific effects of ETS compared to no-
policy intervention scenarios.

In cases where quantitative data is insufficient, the study employs a qualitative assessment to
complement the analysis.

      Stakeholder consultations and expert interviews: to gauge the expected challenges and
          benefits of ETS implementation;

      Comparative policy analysis: to draw lessons from international experiences where similar
          gaps in data existed.

      Case study approaches: to infer potential impacts based on the performance of ETS
          systems in countries with comparable economic structures.

In summary, this study adopts a comprehensive, multi-level assessment framework to evaluate
the impacts of the ETS in Viet Nam. The methodology integrates analyses at three distinct levels-
enterprises, industry, and macroeconomic- to provide a holistic understanding of how ETS
influences different sectors and the broader economy. By ensuring consistency and linkage across
these levels, the study captures sectoral dynamics and their broader economic implications,
forming a robust foundation for policy recommendations.

          is regulated under Decision No. 1480/QD-TTg, issued by the Prime Minister on 29
          November 2022, on the promulgation of the list of key energy users in 2021 (Decision No.

     Data from surveys and studies of other projects: The Consultant made efforts to collect

          data obtained from several ongoing and completed projects, including energy
          consumption and production output data for thermal power plants from the National Grid

          data from the study on Developing GHG emission factors for sub-sectors in building
          material production (cement, ceramic tiles, construction glass) carried out by the Viet Nam
          Institute for Building Materials. Baseline survey data from a project supporting Viet Nam's
          Biennial Transparency Report (BTR) has also been incorporated.

     Data from facility reports: A number of facilities disclose sustainability reports, energy

          audit reports, and annual reports, which include statistical data on fuel consumption and
          GHG emissions.

II.1.1. Greenhouse gas emission profile by sector (micro level)

To assess the impact of GHG emission reduction policies under the NDC and ETS scenarios, it is
essential to accurately determine emissions across economic sectors, with a particular focus on
the three selected industries for the pilot phase (thermal power, steel production, and cement

II.2. Scenario development for modelling and impact assessment

II.2.1. Assumptions of ETS governance options

The analysis of corporate behaviour is based on the assumptions of General Equilibrium Theory
(Arrow & Debreu, 1954)2, where firms make decisions to optimise their economic benefits in a
perfectly competitive environment. The use of General Equilibrium Theory is appropriate for
analysing a closed market because the theory is designed to capture the interdependencies
among agents and sectors within a self-contained economy. In a closed market, where there is no
interaction with external economies, the assumptions of the model (such as market clearing,
rational behaviour, and complete markets) are more likely to hold or can be reasonably
approximated. This allows for a structured and internally consistent analysis of how firms respond
to policy changes, resource constraints, or carbon pricing mechanisms without the added
complexity of international trade, capital flows, or cross-border policy effects. As such, it provides
a solid theoretical foundation for modelling corporate behaviour in Viet Nam's domestic market,
especially when focusing on sectors or policies that are primarily influenced by national
regulations. However, there are specific differences since the traded product in the market is
carbon units (allowances and credits). In this context, the ETS is established under conditions
where multiple buyers and sellers participate, but no single entity can influence market prices.
Prices are determined by supply and demand, all market participants have full knowledge of prices
and influencing factors, and the market operates freely without transaction costs. Firms aim to
maximise profit or minimise costs, and transactions can occur within allowable trading limits
without monopoly power. In the Arrow-Debreu model, firms optimise their profits based on
market prices and adjust production until marginal cost equals marginal revenue. Market
equilibrium is achieved when supply equals demand, ensuring that all firms optimise profits and
consumers maximise their utility at equilibrium prices. If supply and demand are unbalanced,
prices adjust to restore equilibrium.

The limitation of this theory is its assumption of a perfectly competitive market, which is often
unrealistic. In reality, monopolistic behaviours can emerge, and some enterprises may have the
power to influence market prices. Additionally, the model does not account for strategic corporate
behaviours such as predatory pricing. However, for a specialised and relatively small-scale market

2 Arrow, K. J., & Debreu, G. (1954). Existence of an equilibrium for a competitive economy. Econometrica, 22(3), 265-290

like ETS, using this theory for modelling remains appropriate. It provides a structured framework
to analyse price adjustments and equilibrium dynamics while acknowledging the potential
deviations in real-world scenarios.

The assumptions regarding ETS scenarios are developed to analyse corporate behaviour in the
presence and absence of an ETS, while identifying key market factors, including assumptions on
operational mechanisms, emission reduction measures, and investment costs under different
policy scenarios. The analysis process involves steps such as determining the carbon price,
allowance supply and demand, investment costs within and beyond the market scope, total
corporate costs when participating in ETS, starting carbon pricing, and costs for purchasing credits
and allowances from the carbon market. These findings help assess the impact of ETS on achieving
NDC targets and optimising emission reduction strategies.

II.2.1.1. Analysis of corporate behaviour in the presence and absence of ETS

     In the absence of an ETS: It is assumed that enterprises are required to fully invest in

          mitigation measures to achieve the NDC target. If the number of available mitigation
          measures exceeds the necessary amount, enterprises will adhere to the principle of cost
          minimisation. This means they will select mitigation measures in order of increasing total
          investment cost, starting from the lowest-cost options, until the NDC target is met.

     In the presence of ETS:

     (i) Enterprises will prioritise implementing emission reduction measures if the cost of
          implementation is lower than the price of carbon credits on the market. Following the cost
          optimisation principle, firms will adopt mitigation solutions with costs below the market
          price. If the number of available mitigation measures exceeds the necessary amount,
          enterprises will adhere to the principle of cost minimisation; they will select mitigation
          measures starting from the lowest-cost options until the NDC target is met. Besides, they
          could also leverage opportunities to generate profits by selling surplus allowances on the
          ETS. This mechanism helps offset part of the investment costs in mitigation measures,
          enhancing economic efficiency and optimising emission management strategies.

     (ii) If the implemented mitigation measures with costs lower than the market price are still
          insufficient to meet the NDC target, enterprises will opt to purchase offset credits on the
          ETS. This decision enables firms to comply with emission reduction commitments at an
          optimal cost while utilising the ETS as an effective supplementary mechanism when low-
          cost mitigation solutions alone are inadequate to fully achieve the set targets.

     (iii) In cases where both strategies � investing in mitigation measures with costs lower than
          the market price and purchasing offset credits on the ETS � have been implemented but
          the NDC target remains unmet, enterprises are required to consider buying allowances on
          ETS or investing in mitigation measures with costs higher than the market price. At this
          point, the role of the ETS emerges as a supportive mechanism for businesses to reduce
          the costs associated with investing in expensive mitigation measures. However, the supply
          of allowances in the ETS market is limited. Therefore, if the existing supply is sufficient to
          meet allowance demand, firms may find it unnecessary to continue investing. Conversely,
          if the supply falls short of demand, firms will still be required to implement costly

          mitigation measures in order to comply with their NDC targets. However, firms will not
          invest in all high-cost measures due to economic inefficiency. Instead, they will selectively
          adopt mitigation solutions that enable them to achieve the NDC target at the minimum
          total cost, prioritising measures from the lowest to the highest cost. This investment
          process will cease as soon as the targeted GHG reduction level is reached, ensuring
          financial efficiency in emission management.

     (iv) The costs associated with investing in mitigation measures and purchasing
          allowances/carbon credits on the ETS are considered compliance costs rather than direct
          investments that contribute to production and generate added value for the economy. For
          industries selected for pilot implementation, these expenditures are primarily aimed at
          meeting GHG reduction requirements rather than expanding production. From an
          economic perspective, such costs are categorised as regulatory compliance expenses
          rather than profit-generating investments.

II.2.1.2. Assumptions about ETS

The starting carbon price is determined based on the supply-demand allowances, ensuring that
the gap between supply and demand allowances remains minimal. The carbon market is a
specialised market where a unique commodity � allowance � is traded at varying price levels.
These price differences stem from the cost of different emission mitigation measures rather than
solely reflecting market trading behaviour.

The supply of allowances in the ETS market represents the volume of GHG emissions that can be
reduced beyond the NDC target at a cost lower than the market price. In contrast, the demand for
allowances reflects the volume of GHG emissions that must be reduced to meet the NDC target
after all mitigation measures with costs below the market price have been exhausted. At each
price level, enterprises will make different decisions regarding investment or market participation,
which in turn determines a specific supply and demand allowance quantity.

The starting carbon price in the market is the price level that generates a supply greater than
demand with minimal surplus, ensuring that NDC targets are met without driving prices
excessively high. This prevents excessive financial pressure on businesses and mitigates the risk
of market exit:

     If the allowances supply on ETS is insufficient to meet demand, the carbon price will rise.

          When the market price exceeds the cost of implementing mitigation measures, businesses
          may exit the ETS and prioritise direct investments in emission reduction measures. In this
          case, participation in the ETS is not mandatory if businesses can achieve NDC targets
          through internal mitigation strategies.

     Conversely, if supply exceeds demand, the carbon price will decline until it equals or falls

          below the cost of the least expensive mitigation measure that those businesses plan to
          implement. In this scenario, businesses will prioritise purchasing allowances on the ETS
          rather than investing in internal emission reduction measures. This approach allows them
          to minimise compliance costs within the ETS framework.

Thus, the initial carbon price selected for the market falls within the range from the lowest cost of
mitigation measures to the highest-cost measure being implemented. The final starting carbon
price in the market in this simulation is then determined based on the conditions analysed above.

b. Regarding the establishment of the national GHG emissions scenarios

Assumptions are established for three different NDC scenarios in accordance with the
unconditional NDC target, the conditional NDC target, and the conditional NDC target combined
with JETP-based commitment. These scenarios reflect the levels of emission reduction ambition of
Viet Nam in the context of socio-economic development, depending on domestic resources and
international support.

      In the Business as Usual (BAU) scenario, Viet Nam does not implement any strong emission
          reduction measures beyond current policies. In this case, GHG emissions would increase
          in line with economic growth and energy demand, reaching 726.2 million tCOe by 2025,
          and 927.9 million tCOe by 2030.

      The Unconditional Contribution scenario reflects Viet Nam's commitment to reducing
          emissions using domestic resources, without relying on international financial or
          technological support. In this scenario, Viet Nam implements measures such as improving
          energy efficiency and developing renewable energy within the scope of domestic financial
          capabilities. As a result, GHG emissions would be reduced to 635.2 million tCOe by 2025,
          and 781.6 million tCOe by 2030 (a 15.8% reduction).

      The Conditional Contribution scenario assumes that Viet Nam receives financial,
          technological, and capacity-building support from international sources. In this case,
          emission reduction measures will be more robust, including expanding renewable energy,
          gradually reducing dependence on fossil fuels, and improving efficiency in industries. With
          this support, GHG emissions could be reduced to 520.4 million tCOe by 2025 (a 28.3%
          reduction compared to BAU), and 524.2 million tCOe by 2030 (a 43.5% reduction).

      The Conditional Contribution with JETP scenario is based on the assumption that Viet Nam
          would receive stronger support from the international community, including the IPG, and
          the GFANZ Working Group members under JETP initiative to accelerate action towards the
          objectives and long-term goals of the UNFCCC and the Paris Agreement and to support a
          just energy transition. With JETP, Viet Nam could implement emission reduction measures
          more rapidly, such as increasing investment in renewable energy and halting the
          development of coal-fired power plants. Under this scenario, GHG emissions would be
          reduced to 520.4 million tCOe by 2025, and 509.1 million tCOe by 2030 (a 45.1%
          reduction).

Thus, the Conditional Contribution + JETP scenario represents Viet Nam's maximum emission
reduction potential, contingent on receiving adequate international support, particularly in
facilitating the transition to clean energy.

c. Regarding the establishment of ETS emission caps according to different NDC scenarios

The emission cap in each scenario is established by subtracting the contribution of GHG emission
reduction targets, derived from various NDC scenarios, from the projected GHG emissions under
the BAU scenario. This reduction reflects the cumulative impact of relevant mitigation measures.

For example, the electricity sector cap is calculated as:

Electricity sector cap = Electricity sector emissions in BAU scenario - Emission reduction target of
the electricity sector (i.e. total emission reduction contribution from relevant electricity sector
measures)

Meanwhile, the cap in the BAU scenario is determined by considering the coverage ratio of the
ETS and the emission share of each sector within the ETS framework.

II.2.2.2. Identify mitigation measures

Based on the technical characteristics of each pilot sector, the appropriate GHG reduction
measures were selected based on the following information:

      Emission reduction potential (GHG): The amount of GHGs reduced reflects the annual
          impact of implementing mitigation measures within the pilot sector. As investments are
          made annually, the emission reduction potential of each measure will increase
          progressively over time, contributing to greater cumulative reductions.

      Investment cost (ci): The cost and financial needs associated with each mitigation measure
          are estimated based on the average cost per ton of CO emissions reduced, as outlined in
          the NDC Technical Report. These estimates provide insight into the investment
          requirements for achieving the targeted emission reductions under each scenario. For
          measures under the JETP, particularly those related to reductions in coal-fired power
          generation and supporting energy transition, cost data are referenced from published
          studies. Specifically, the investment cost for the supercritical technology is estimated at
          140 USD/tCO, while JETP energy efficiency measures are assumed to be equal to E16,
          improving energy efficiency in industrial subsectors (2.03 USD/tCO2).

      While the NDC Technical Report serves as the primary source for investment cost
          estimates, in several cases, it does not provide complete or up-to-date information for
          certain mitigation measures, particularly newer technologies or measures introduced
          under international partnerships such as JETP. Therefore, additional reputable sources,
          including international studies (e.g., IEA Energy Technology Perspectives) and updated
          project-level data (e.g., from TransitionZero), were used to supplement gaps and ensure
          the comprehensiveness and accuracy of the investment cost assumptions used in the
          modelling.

Emission reduction measures can be broadly divided into three main groups based on their cost-
effectiveness and implementation impacts. The high-efficiency, low-cost group includes measures
that offer significant emission reductions at relatively low cost, such as the use of cement additives,
reducing heat loss, and improving energy efficiency in industry. The medium-cost group includes
measures that require moderate investment but offer substantial long-term benefits. Examples
are renewable technologies such as solar power, onshore wind power, and improvements in
cement and steel production. Finally, the high-cost but strategic group includes measures that are
more capital-intensive but essential for achieving long-term emission reduction goals and
supporting the broader energy transition. Examples include coal-fired power elimination, offshore
wind development, and steel production technology upgrades. Specifically:

      GHG emission reduction measures differ significantly in terms of investment costs and
          implementation feasibility across key industrial sectors. In the thermal power sector, low-
          cost, high-efficiency measures include improving energy efficiency in industry (2.03
          USD/tCO) and developing small hydropower plants (1.53 USD/tCO), as they make use of
          available resources with reasonable investment. Medium-cost measures, such as
          concentrated solar power (3.39 USD/tCO) or supercritical thermal power technology (3.67
          USD/tCO), help improve electricity generation efficiency, although their deployment may
          be constrained by intermittent weather conditions or continued reliance on fossil fuels.
          Meanwhile, solutions such as offshore wind power (12.66 USD/tCO) or the elimination of

         coal-fired power (140 USD/tCO) are high-cost but play a crucial role in long-term carbon-
         neutral strategies.
     In the cement industry, low-cost and readily implementable measures such as using
         industrial waste additives to replace clinker (0.05 USD/tCO) or reducing clinker kiln heat
         loss (0.11 USD/tCO) yield significant results without requiring large investments. Other
         measures, such as optimising the clinker firing process (1.12 USD/tCO) or recovering
         waste heat from cement production (1.62 USD/tCO), help save energy but require
         technology upgrades. However, solutions like using vertical mills (10.65 USD/tCO) offer
         substantial benefits but demand significant capital investment.
     In the steel production sector, some low-cost solutions, such as injecting powdered
         anthracite coal into blast furnaces (0.82 USD/tCO), can be quickly implemented to
         improve furnace efficiency. Medium-cost measures, such as preheating in the steel rolling
         mill (2.62 USD/tCO) or improving BOF technology (2.41 USD/tCO), significantly reduce
         energy consumption in the steelmaking process. However, high-cost solutions, such as
         preheating scrap steel before charging it into electric arc furnaces (3.78 USD/tCO) or
         improving steel industry performance (12.71 USD/tCO), require major changes in the
         production process.

The facilities included in the modelling analysis for ETS governance options, as outlined in previous

III.1.2. Investment costs for mitigation measures

This section examines the investment costs required to implement GHG mitigation measures
under different NDC scenarios, with and without the application of an ETS. It begins with an
overview of cost trends across scenarios, highlighting how the ETS reduces overall compliance

III.1.2.1. Overview of ETS impacts on investment costs

As analysed in the context of corporate behaviour, the presence of an ETS offers a significant
opportunity for cost optimisation in achieving NDC targets compared to scenarios without an ETS.
Model evaluation results clearly indicate that total compliance costs associated with NDC
implementation are significantly reduced across all scenarios when ETS is in place, compared to
scenarios without such a mechanism.

In the absence of an ETS, the estimated investment costs for GHG mitigation measures from 2025
to 2030 are estimated at 420.5 million USD under the unconditional NDC scenario. However, these
costs increase substantially under more ambitious policy pathways. Specifically, under the
conditional NDC scenario, total compliance costs rise to 1,297 million USD. In the conditional NDC
scenario combined with JETP, costs further increase to 1,331 million USD.

The escalation in compliance costs across these scenarios clearly illustrates that stricter GHG
emission reduction requirements compel enterprises to adopt more extensive and costly

mitigation measures. This highlights the economic burden associated with stricter NDC
commitments when no market-based flexibility is available.

reflects the inherent challenges associated with meeting more stringent climate commitments,
including the need for widespread adoption of advanced and capital-intensive mitigation
technologies.

Participation in the ETS enables businesses to optimise compliance costs, especially in scenarios
with tighter emission constraints. Nevertheless, when NDC targets become more demanding,
businesses are often required to adopt multiple costly mitigation measures, leading to a significant
increase in total compliance expenditures. This highlights the critical role of the ETS in supporting
businesses to achieve emission targets at the lowest possible cost.

Among the various measures, investment costs under the unconditional ETS20 scenario are the
lowest, followed by the unconditional ETS10, while the highest compliance costs are observed in
the scenario without an ETS, where businesses must independently meet the more rigorous
requirements of the conditional NDC and JETP pathways. This comparison clearly demonstrates
the economic efficiency and strategic value of incorporating an ETS in Viet Nam's climate policy
framework.

III.2. Impact on the economy

This section assesses the macroeconomic implications of implementing the ETS using a structural
econometric model to analyse key indicators such as GDP, investment, consumption, and
consumer prices. A central assumption of the model is that compliance costs, i.e. expenditures on
emission reduction measures, are treated as investments that do not generate direct added value,
but are instead directed toward achieving environmental objectives. According to Keynesian
aggregate demand theory, if businesses are required to shift investment resources from
production to compliance costs, especially in high-emission sectors like thermal power, cement,
and steel, the total social investment capital will decrease, leading to a negative impact on GDP.
The sectoral reduction in productive investment is introduced into the structural econometric
model used in this analysis. This model, which integrates both supply- and demand-side dynamics,
allows for the simulation of longer-term feedback effects, including interactions among key
supply-side factors (such as productivity, labour, and capital formation) and demand-side
components (such as consumption, investment, and expenditure).

This argument is supported by several international studies. Wolfgang Mostert (2022) points out
that high compliance costs can lead to 'investment diversion,' meaning businesses may delay or
reduce expansion investments if there are no policies supporting technological innovation or
reinvestment in lower-emission sectors. The 'Carbon Pricing Watch 2016' report by the World Bank
Group Climate Change also warns that carbon pricing, especially in the context of unstable price
signals or high transaction costs, can significantly impact investment dynamics, causing businesses
in regulated sectors to delay or scale back their investment plans.

The input data for the structural econometric model in this study includes time series on the
investment scale of three major emitting sectors � thermal power, cement, and steel � under

scenarios with and without the emissions trading system. The results clearly demonstrate the
regulatory impact of ETS on investment through compliance costs.

Annual investment costs fluctuate depending on the investment cost of each mitigation measure,
the choice of measures, the NDC targets, and the demand for allowance/credit purchases in the
market. Accordingly, differences in investment costs and NDC targets lead to different selections
of mitigation options, resulting in variations in annual investment costs. In some sectors, during
the pilot year, a greater number of mitigation measures must be implemented to meet the NDC
target due to the lower emissions reduction potential of available options. Nevertheless, as the
NDC targets become increasingly stringent, the overall trend of investment costs continues to rise,
particularly from 2028 onward.

In the unconditional NDC scenario, the total cost fluctuates from about 66.9 million USD,
accounting for approximately 0.49% of the total investment of the three sectors (in 2025), to 74.3
million USD, accounting for about 0.43% of the total investment of the three sectors (in 2030).
Meanwhile, under the conditional scenario and the conditional scenario with JETP, the total costs
are 231.6 million USD and 242.8 million USD by 2030, accounting for about 1.36% and 1.42% of
the total investment of the three sectors.

When applying the ETS scenario with a maximum offset limit of 10%, compliance costs are better
regulated. In the unconditional case, costs fluctuate, ranging from 10.1 million USD to 5.1 million
USD during the 2025�2030 period (accounting for about 0.03%�0.07% of the total investment of
the three sectors), there is significant decrease of investment costs over time when it is possible
to offset the carbon allowance by purchasing credit on market. However, in the conditional and
conditional with JETP scenarios, the total cost is around 122.1 million USD and 157.9 million USD
by 2030, increasing from 104.8 million USD in 2025, respectively, accounting for about 0.8%-0.9%
of the total investment of the three sectors by 2030.

In the ETS scenario with a 20% offset limit, the costs for the three sectors in the unconditional case
remain at the same low levels as in the scenario with the 10% offset limit, but investment in the
conditional and conditional with JETP scenarios decreases further. The compliance cost is 3.1
million USD in 2025 and up to 4.1 million USD in 2030 in unconditional NDC. Specifically, the cost
by 2030 in the conditional and conditional with JETP scenarios is approximately 122.1 million USD
from 82.6 million USD, respectively, accounting for about 0.6-0.7% of the total investment of the
three sectors.

These results confirm that while compliance costs inevitably increase with more ambitious
emission targets, the relative burden remains modest, even under the most stringent scenarios,
when measured as a share of total sectoral investment. More importantly, the implementation of
an ETS, especially with a higher offset limit, plays a key role in moderating these costs, thereby
mitigating potential negative impacts on economic growth. The ETS mechanism thus emerges as
a strategic policy tool to balance environmental goals with macroeconomic stability.

compliance costs leads to decreased demand for goods and services, which in turn reduces

The modelling results confirm that emission reduction policies may have a limited negative impact
on economic growth. However, this effect remains manageable if the ETS policy is designed with
flexibility, particularly through the use of offset credits generated by emission reduction projects,
supported by international cooperation, and effectively integrated with global carbon market
mechanisms. Such an approach enables Viet Nam to pursue its climate commitments sustainably
while preserving short-term economic growth.

The econometric analysis presented in this study provides crucial quantitative insights for
designing optimal offset limits, empowering Viet Nam to fully harness the benefits of domestic
and international carbon markets. Over the long term, a well-coordinated implementation of the
ETS, alongside financial mechanisms such as JETP, will not only mitigate adverse GDP impacts but
also strengthen the foundation for a resilient, net-zero emissions trajectory, aligned with
sustainable development goals.

The findings of this study are largely consistent with several international studies assessing the
impact of ETS. Recent research on ETS implementations in China, South Korea, the European
Union (EU), and specific industries has demonstrated that ETS can contribute to GHG emission
reductions without undermining macroeconomic indicators. In fact, ETS can even facilitate
productivity improvements and promote investment.

In the study by Jin et al. (2020), a Computable General Equilibrium model revealed that the
nationwide deployment of ETS in China � covering 10 high-emission sectors and utilising an
output-based allocation (OBA) mechanism � enabled the achievement of NDC targets with
relatively minimal GDP losses, ranging from only 0.07% to 0.14%, depending on the stringency of
the allocation scheme. Similarly, the 2024 Progress Report by China's Ministry of Ecology and
Environment highlighted that the national ETS now covers approximately 5.1 billion tons of CO
annually, accounting for over 40% of the country's total emissions. This establishes the world's
largest carbon pricing platform, supporting a cost-effective energy transition.

At the city level, Wang et al. (2023), using a spatial difference-in-differences (spatial DID) model on
data from 284 cities from 2003 to 2019, found that the pilot ETS policy significantly improved
carbon emission efficiency (CEE), particularly in eastern urban areas and traditional industrial
hubs. However, negative spillover effects were observed in neighbouring cities not participating in

the ETS, although this impact diminished markedly when the distance exceeded 900 km. In South
Korea, a study by O. et al. (2023) indicated that during the initial phase of ETS implementation
(2015�2017), listed companies did not achieve significant emission reductions. Nevertheless, the
overall energy efficiency of the sector improved due to the flexible use of emission permits and
offset mechanisms.

For the EU, the study by Dechezlepr�tre et al. (2023) indicated that during the first two phases of
the EU ETS (2005�2012), manufacturing facilities reduced CO emissions by an average of
approximately 10%, with the most pronounced effect observed during the 2008�2012 period. At
the same time, firms within the system experienced growth in revenues and fixed assets, while
profits and employment were not significantly impacted. This demonstrates that the ETS can
achieve environmental objectives while maintaining competitiveness and fostering technological
innovation.

In the context of China's steel industry, the study by Duan et al. (2019) applied a two-stage dynamic
game model and found that when the emission reduction target was increased from 15% to 30%,
both social welfare and producer surplus improved, while macroeconomic losses gradually
decreased. This finding reinforces the effectiveness and feasibility of market-based carbon pricing
policies.

In addition, the simulation report by IETA (2023) indicates that if all countries cooperate to
implement their NDCs through the Article 6 mechanism of the Paris Agreement, the
implementation costs could be reduced by up to 50% compared to unilateral approaches. If the
cost savings are reinvested to enhance climate ambitions, the global emission reduction level
could be doubled, thereby creating a crucial incentive for forming "ambition clubs" and increasing
the overall system's efficiency.

III.2.2. Impact on sectoral structural shift

The implementation of the ETS provides businesses with the flexibility to select suitable
production and business options, enabling cost optimisation while fulfilling GHG emission
reduction commitments under the NDC. However, ETS will also impact the sectoral structure.
Specifically, high-emission industries such as coal-fired power generation, cement, and steel
production will face pressure to reduce emissions or transition to cleaner technologies. In
contrast, low-emission and green technology sectors, including renewable energy, electrified
transportation, energy-efficient manufacturing, and agroforestry, will be better positioned for
growth. This change will drive a structural shift in the economy, reallocating capital and labour
toward more sustainable industries.

Measures such as the development of biogas power (E36), waste-to-energy (E35, E34), biomass
power (E33), and solar power (E29, E30) contribute to promoting renewable energy, attracting
investment, creating new jobs, and transitioning the economic model towards sustainability and
green growth. Meanwhile, advancements in the cement and steel industries (E8, E7, I2, I1, I4s) help
optimise production, reduce dependence on raw materials, and enhance circularity. However, this
process requires traditional businesses to make significant investments in new technologies,
leading to higher short-term costs.

The development of solar power, waste-to-energy, biomass power, and biogas power reduces
dependence on coal-fired and oil and gas power, promoting the use of clean energy. Measures to

enhance energy efficiency in the industrial sector (E16, ETP) help reduce fossil fuel consumption.
However, this shift may put pressure on the coal and oil and gas industries due to decreased
demand.

Overall, the comparison between scenarios indicates that sectoral shifts toward low-carbon
technologies and production structures occur more rapidly under ETS implementation than under
the BAU scenario. By placing sectors under the ETS, the policy creates stronger economic
incentives for emission reduction and resource allocation, driving a faster transition to cleaner and
more efficient technologies. This market-based approach not only accelerates structural
transformation but also offers a more cost-effective pathway for achieving Viet Nam's NDC targets,
compared to relying solely on individual, isolated efforts of covered sectors without the support
of a coordinated trading mechanism.

technology and investment options, but lacks the flexibility and cost-optimisation benefits of a

     E34. Developing waste-to-energy power generation through incineration
     E28. Developing small hydropower
     E29. Developing concentrated solar power
     E30. Developing rooftop solar power

The implementation of these mitigation measures has a profound impact on the structure of
related sectors, including:

      The energy and power sector: Traditional power generation industries will face
          competitive pressure from renewable energy sources, forcing them to either adapt or
          optimise performance. The development of solar power, biomass energy, and biogas
          power will reduce dependence on fossil fuels. However, this transition will impact the
          sector's workforce, as workers in traditional industries may be required to change
          professions, while the emerging sectors demand highly skilled technical expertise,
          necessitating a period of adjustment. Enterprises will gradually shift to sustainable
          production models, adhering to low-emission standards. Many manufacturing and
          industrial enterprises will benefit from access to clean energy, meeting market demands,
          particularly those engaged in international trade and global value chains.

      The agriculture and biomass sector: Generating revenue from agricultural by-products
          through the development of biomass power and biogas power will create new income
          streams from materials such as rice straw, rice husks, and sawdust. This approach will
          drive an internal shift within the agricultural sector toward promoting sustainable farming

          practices. Farmers can supply raw materials for biomass and biogas power production
          while also utilising agricultural waste effectively.
      Internal labour market shifts: The demand for renewable energy engineers, solar power
          technicians, and waste management specialists is expected to surge. Meanwhile, workers
          in the coal and oil sectors will need to undergo retraining to meet new market demands.

      E8. Reducing heat losses in clinker kilns.
      E7. Optimising the clinker combustion process.
      I2. Using industrial waste by-products as additives to replace clinker (e.g., blast furnace

          slag, fly ash).
      I1. Using natural mineral additives to replace clinker (e.g., limestone, pozzolan).

The implementation of energy-saving measures and green production practices drives the
structural shift in the cement industry from traditional production models to high-tech,
sustainable, and low-emission production. This transformation alters the industry's supply chain,
requiring suppliers of additives to meet high-quality and environmentally friendly standards.
Cement enterprises are increasingly transitioning from traditional plants to integrated green
manufacturing facilities that utilise renewable energy and waste heat recovery technologies.
Moreover, policies promoting the use of green building materials create incentives for companies
in the sector to expand their market share into international markets with stringent environmental
requirements.

For the BOF steel industry, some priority measures that can be implemented include:

      E13. Preheating in the steel rolling mill
      E15. Injecting pulverised anthracite coal into the blast furnace
      E14. Recovering waste heat from the BOF

The implementation of energy-saving measures and the development of advanced technologies
drive the steel industry to shift from traditional production methods to greener, low-emission, and
more energy-efficient manufacturing. This transition not only enhances competitiveness but also
enables Vietnamese steel enterprises to meet stringent international standards on emissions and
environmental protection, facilitating access to high-end markets such as the EU, the US, and
Japan. Domestic demand for goods may also evolve, with a stronger focus on high-quality,
environmentally friendly steel products that meet more rigorous technical requirements.

However, these measures will also impact the workforce within the industry. New technologies
require engineers and workers to have in-depth knowledge of automation, industrial control, and
energy management. Regular technical training must be organised to keep employees updated
on new technologies, particularly in the management and operation of energy optimisation
systems. The industry will shift from manual labour to technical management roles with skills in
digitalisation and data management. Stricter occupational safety requirements will be enforced,
with rigorous standards for incident handling and environmental protection.

For EAF steel industry, some priority measures that can be applied include:

      E13. Reheating in the rolling mill
      E12. Preheating scrap steel before charging it into EAF

The implementation of measures E13 and E12 in the EAF steel sector will lead to significant
structural changes, such as:

      Increased demand for high-quality scrap steel: The implementation of measure E12
          involves preheating scrap steel before feeding it into the EAF furnace, which reduces
          energy consumption during melting and improves production efficiency. This approach
          encourages the procurement of high-quality, low-impurity scrap steel to maximise melting
          efficiency. As a result, demand for scrap steel rises, prompting steel enterprises to
          establish stable supply chains for recycled materials from domestic and imported sources.

      Enhanced EAF operation and preheating system skills: Workers will require specialised
          training in control techniques, energy management, and maintenance of the scrap steel
          preheating system and EAF furnace. Manual tasks will increasingly be replaced by
          automation and remote control systems, necessitating proficiency in automation
          management, digital skills, and data processing. The demand for quality control engineers
          specialising in scrap steel assessment and personnel for scrap collection and sorting will
          also rise to meet the growing demand.

Opportunities for international market access: The adoption of EAF technology with preheating
enables the steel sector to meet stringent emission and energy efficiency standards set by the EU,
US, and Japan. This compliance positions Vietnamese steel producers to access high-value
international markets, strengthen export competitiveness, and cater to the growing demand for
low-carbon, sustainable steel products.

          In the absence of an ETS, to achieve the conditional NDC, conditional NDC with JETP

targets, the pilot sectors � particularly the thermal power sector � must implement additional
measures such as E38 (development of ultra-supercritical thermal power technology), E37
(development of LNG-fueled combined cycle gas turbines), and E31 (onshore wind power
development) to meet NDC targets. For the steel sector, it is mandatory to adopt ETP (energy
efficiency on the demand side for BOF steel) and I4s (application of best available technologies to
reduce emissions in the steel sector through BOF technology improvements) to fulfil NDC
requirements.

The implementation of measure E38 (development of ultra-supercritical thermal power
technology - USC) enhances thermal power plant efficiency by utilising steam at extremely high
pressure and temperature (above 600�C and 30 MPa). Plant efficiency can reach 45-50%,
compared to only about 35-40% in conventional thermal power plants. Consequently, this reduces
fuel consumption, lowers electricity production costs, and decreases GHG emissions. However, it
requires high-quality coal, skilled technical labour, and potential retrofitting or replacement of
existing thermal power plants to adopt the technology.

The implementation of measure E37 (development of LNG-based combined cycle gas turbines)
achieves higher efficiency compared to traditional fuels, reaching up to 60%. This leads to a
significant increase in demand for natural gas, requiring a stable supply chain, import
infrastructure, and storage facilities. It also necessitates higher technical skills and specialised
expertise for workers in the management and operation of the technology.

The implementation of measure E31 (onshore wind power development) will increase the demand
for energy storage technologies (ESS) to store excess electricity generated from wind power. The

workforce shift will require a skilled team specialising in renewable energy and complex grid
control systems.

          In the case of an ETS, within three pilot sectors, the key difference compared to the non-

ETS scenario is that businesses have more options, rather than investing entirely in all mitigation
measures directly, but can instead partially meet their obligations through credit trading.
However, since the adoption of mitigation technologies is more selective, the pace of sectoral and
labour market transition is slower, and the overall structural shift within industries remains
comparable to the non-ETS scenario, though less immediate.

For the coal-fired power sector, under the unconditional NDC scenario, in the first year, the
industry would only need to apply measures. Consequently, sectoral and labour transitions would
occur primarily due to the influence of this measure only, such as promoting renewable energy
with small hydropower. Once the NDC target is met, and if the investment cost exceeds the market
price, businesses would lack incentives to implement additional mitigation measures. Should the
NDC targets become more stringent, greenhouse gas mitigation measures will be increasingly
adopted as carbon pricing escalates. Technologies such as biomass, waste-to-energy, and
concentrated solar power will then be deployed, accelerating the shift of the energy sector toward
cleaner sources relative to the unconditional scenario.

In the case of the gas-fired power sector, no mitigation measures would need to be implemented
if an ETS is in place under the unconditional NDC scenario. As a result, the shift toward green
energy would not occur. In both the coal-fired and gas-fired power sectors, developing onshore
wind power (E31) is considered a costly option. Therefore, the preferred approach would be to
purchase credits/allowances from the market, which would be sufficient to meet the NDC target.
Consequently, the development of onshore wind power would not be driven as it would be in the
absence of an ETS. In the case that NDC targets are tightened, GHG emission reduction measures
will be more extensively employed as carbon prices rise. At that point, solutions such as E16, E28,
E29, and E33 will be implemented, contributing to a faster transition of the energy sector toward
cleaner sources compared to the unconditional scenario.

In the cement manufacturing sector, which offers many large-scale and cost-effective solutions for
GHG emissions reduction, this industry would act as a supplier of allowances to the market.
Consequently, the cement industry is expected to maintain the full implementation of mitigation
measures as it would in the absence of an ETS. The sector's internal restructuring is projected to
proceed along a similar path to the non-ETS baseline scenario.

In the BOF steel sector, in the unconditional NDC scenario, they would only apply E15 instead of
E13 and E14 compared to the non-ETS scenario, since they could purchase the remaining GHG on
the market. This option is slower than the sectoral structural shifts. However, in the case that NDC
targets are tightened, the structural shift within the industry remains unchanged compared to the
scenario without an ETS, as all mitigation measures continue to be implemented. This sector acts
as a supplier of allowances in the market due to the low cost of implementing measures compared
to the starting carbon price.

Conversely, in the EAF steel sector, the structural shift only occurs with measure E13, as measure
E12 is not required. Instead, the sector opts to purchase credits/allowances in the ETS. As a result,
the pace of structural change in the EAF steel sector will be slower.

           In the case of an ETS, a financial mechanism enables the transfer of resources from
high-emission sectors to those capable of generating marketable credits or allowances, thereby
facilitating the shift toward greener and more sustainable industries. Under the selected initial
carbon price level used for simulation, a portion of financial resources from the thermal power
and steel sectors has been redirected to carbon credit-generating projects via the credit market,
and to the cement sector through the ETS. Specifically, the thermal power sector is required to
transfer between 69 million USD and up to 337.6 million USD to the cement sector and credit-
generating projects through the ETS. This represents the compliance cost borne by the thermal
power sector to meet its NDC targets, while simultaneously serving as a significant revenue stream
for the cement sector and carbon credit providers. This dynamic illustrates a capital reallocation
from high-emission sectors to those with greater mitigation potential or credit-generation
capacity. Accordingly, the ETS facilitates a more transparent and targeted flow of capital toward
greener industries.

III.3. Overall assessment across different governance options

The choice of the NDC implementation scenario will directly determine the cost of emission
reduction and the macroeconomic impact on Viet Nam in the coming period. If the no-ETS scenario
is chosen, the primary advantage lies in the ability to rapidly implement the plan without the need
to build carbon market infrastructure or complex monitoring systems. This approach may be
suitable if Viet Nam seeks to retain full domestic control and is not yet prepared to integrate with
the international carbon market. However, this option entails the highest compliance cost,
reaching 1,297 � 1,331 million USD in the conditional and conditional with JETP scenarios, and
generates the greatest negative impact on GDP, with a projected reduction of up to -0.0208% over
the 2025-2030 period.

In contrast, the ETS10 scenario presents a more cost-effective pathway. It reduces total compliance
costs to approximately 701.3 million USD in the conditional scenario and 769.8 million USD in the
conditional with JETP scenario, while lessening the negative impact on GDP to -0.011% and -0.012%
respectively.

Finally, the ETS20 scenario, which allows a higher offset limit (20%), is considered the most optimal
in terms of cost and economic efficiency. This flexibility enables businesses to choose between
internal abatement or purchasing carbon credits/allowances from the market. As a result, total
compliance costs decrease significantly to about 21.5 million USD, and the negative impact on GDP
is only around -0.0003% in the unconditional scenario. Meanwhile, in the conditional scenario
combined with JETP, the total costs fall to 598.3 million USD, and the GDP decline is limited to -
0.0093% for the entire period of 2025-2030.

IV. CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

IV.1. Key results

This study applies a comprehensive, three-level integrated modelling approach � comprising
micro, sectoral, and macroeconomic analyses � to evaluate the cost-effectiveness and feasibility
of various policy options for implementing the ETS in compliance with Viet Nam's NDC targets.
The assessment focuses on three pilot sectors: thermal power, cement, and steel, and considers
nine policy scenarios, ranging from no ETS to ETS schemes with offset flexibility (ETS10 and
ETS20), across three NDC commitment levels: unconditional, conditional, and conditional with
JETP support.

At the micro level, the study allocates allowances and assesses corporate investment behaviour
under ETS and non-ETS scenarios. The sectoral level examines the impact of the ETS on
technology, compliance costs, and investment decisions. These results are then integrated into
the macro level to quantify the ETS's impacts on GDP, investment, household consumption,
inflation and structural shifts in the economy.

Unlike conventional commodity markets, in the ETS, businesses can opt out of the market by
investing in emission reduction measures, reflecting a comparative advantage in optimising
compliance costs.

The study employs a structured econometric model to quantify the impact of the ETS on the
economy. This model simulates the responses of businesses and consumers to changes in
emission costs, assesses spillover effects on industries and the labour market, and analyses the
effectiveness of policies in balancing economic growth with NDC targets.

Some key findings of the study are as follows:

      The role of ETS in achieving NDC goals: The study confirms that the ETS is a vital tool for
          Viet Nam to achieve its emission reduction targets cost-effectively while supporting a
          sustainable economic transition. The ETS provides businesses with flexibility to choose
          between internal emission reductions or purchasing allowances and credits in the
          carbon market, reflecting comparative advantages in cost optimisation.

      Market price: During the pilot phase, the analysis results suggest that the starting
          carbon price might vary from 1.1 to 3.7 USD per ton of CO2. In the conditional NDC
          scenario, higher demand for credits results in a slight price increase compared to the
          unconditional NDC scenario. Expanding the offset limit to 20% of emission allowances
          (ETS20) might reduce the carbon price on the market. In the ETS20 scenario without
          NDC conditionality, the carbon price is projected to be the lowest due to the significant
          imbalance between supply and demand.

      Compliance costs: ETS significantly reduces investment costs for businesses, particularly
          in conditional NDC scenarios. In the unconditional NDC, investment costs decrease from
          420 million USD to 21.5 million USD with ETS implementation. In the conditional NDC,
          ETS20 lowers investment costs from 1,297 million USD to 598.3 million USD. In total,
          under the unconditional NDC scenario, compliance costs decrease from 420 million USD
          to 164.2 million USD with the implementation of the ETS, while in the conditional NDC
          scenario, costs drop from 1.297 billion USD to 927.6 million USD.

      Sectoral impacts: The power sector benefits the most from ETS due to substantial cost
          reductions, while the cement sector becomes an allowance supplier on the ETS at the
          starting price level. The steel sector experiences minimal impact from trading limits,
          with notable differences among scenarios. ETS enables companies to optimise
          compliance costs and generate revenue from allowance trading. In conditional NDC and
          JETP scenarios, raising the offset limit from 10% to 20% significantly increases both
          credit purchase costs and allowance sale revenue. Power plants are the primary buyers
          due to high mitigation costs, while the cement sector serves as a net seller owing to
          lower mitigation costs.

      Macroeconomic benefits of ETS: Although the impact of meeting NDC targets on
          macroeconomic indicators is limited, ETS mitigates the adverse effects of emission
          reduction policies on GDP, investment, consumption, and consumer prices. Expanding
          the offset limit from 10% to 20% further limits economic downturns, while international
          mechanisms like JETP help Viet Nam meet climate commitments without significant
          economic disruption. ETS not only reduces emissions but also drives economic
          restructuring. High-emission sectors like power, cement, and steel face pressure to
          adopt cleaner technologies, while renewable energy and electrified transportation
          sectors gain growth opportunities. ETS also generates government revenue from
          auctioning allowances and credit trading, though allowances are freely allocated during
          the early market phase.

      Consumption and consumer price: Initially, ETS has a mild inflationary impact due to
          economic adjustments, but the effect is significantly lower than in scenarios without
          ETS. Over the long term, productivity improvements in emission-intensive sectors
          compensate for the initial impact. ETS stabilises the economy by supporting a green
          transition.

      Structural shifts: ETS enables the transfer of financial resources from high-emission
          sectors to those capable of generating marketable credits or allowances, thereby
          facilitating the shift toward greener and more sustainable industries.

Optimal policy choices for NDC implementation: In the unconditional NDC scenario, choosing
ETS20 will offer cost-effective solutions with minimal negative impacts. In the conditional NDC
scenario, a robust carbon market is essential to help businesses reduce compliance costs and
facilitate efficient carbon trading. ETS20 emerges as the most effective policy, providing cost
savings of up to 700 million USD compared to no-ETS scenarios and minimising GDP impact
from -0.02% to -0.009% over 2025-2030. The analysis results suggest that a starting carbon price
could vary from 1.1 to 3.7 USD per ton of CO2, with further adjustments determined by market
mechanisms or through auctioning. Finally, the choice of NDC implementation scenario directly
affects emission reduction costs and economic impacts. Not applying ETS enables rapid
implementation but comes with high costs and misses out on the benefits of the carbon market.
In contrast, ETS10 helps reduce costs and provides an opportunity to become familiar with the
market, while ETS20 offers optimal effectiveness by lowering compliance costs, minimising GDP
impacts, and facilitating international connectivity.

In summary, ETS not only helps Viet Nam achieve emission reduction targets at the lowest cost
and mitigates the growth slowdown from implementing the NDC, but also promotes green

growth, enhances business competitiveness, and ensures sustainable development in the long
term.

     1. Bilateral meetings with line ministries overseeing the pilot sectors � cement, iron and

     2. A multi-stakeholder consultation workshop.

IV.2. Policy recommendation

Based on the results of the quantitative analysis of policy scenarios within the scope of this
study, the Consultant recommends prioritising options that implement ETS, with flexible offset
limits (10-20%), in order to achieve optimal cost-effectiveness and minimise negative impacts
on the economy and society while fulfilling NDC commitments.

Specifically, ETS20 option within the unconditional NDC scenario is identified as the top policy
priority. This option demonstrates low compliance costs, minimal impacts on GDP,
consumption, and prices. Therefore, ETS20 is the appropriate option as Viet Nam aims to fulfil
its NDC commitments with international support and build a carbon finance mechanism linked
to the global market.

Although non-ETS options may be simpler to operate in the early stages, the research results
indicate that they involve higher compliance costs and greater macroeconomic impacts, while
failing to generate market incentives or provide flexibility for businesses.

Designing a flexible ETS policy with offset levels ranging from 10% to 20% is the most suitable
option for the initial phase of NDC implementation and the carbon market in Viet Nam. The
specific option should be selected based on the level of international commitment, institutional
readiness, and the need to balance compliance costs with the ability to generate financial
resources. In the short term, ETS20 within the unconditional NDC scenario could be the most
feasible and effective pilot option, laying a solid foundation for market expansion and gradually
increasing commitment levels in subsequent phases.

IV.3. Some limitations in the study

One of the major challenges in assessing the impact of the ETS in Viet Nam is the limitation of
available data for analysis. To ensure accuracy and effectiveness in allocating emission
allowances, direct collection and validation of emission data at the facility level is a crucial step.
However, this process faces significant financial and time constraints, especially in the context
of limited resources and an underdeveloped emissions monitoring system. As a result, the
study relied on existing secondary data sources from environmental reports, industrial surveys,
and databases from MAE (formerly MONRE). While these sources provide a useful overview,

discrepancies may arise with actual facility-level data. Using multiple data sources for the same
indicator can introduce certain risks, such as inconsistencies in definitions, data collection
methodologies, or update timelines, which may affect the reliability and consistency of the
analysis. These discrepancies may affect the determination of emission allowances allocated to
businesses, altering the maximum volume of credits eligible for offsetting, which in turn impacts
the calculation of the minimum costs businesses need to incur to meet NDC emission reduction
targets. Accurate data collection at facilities not only helps build a comprehensive database that
accurately reflects the emissions situation of each sector but also facilitates monitoring and
enforcement of ETS compliance by businesses during the pilot and official implementation
phases. For modelling, the macroeconomic data used in this report, we rely solely on a single,
consistent and official source (e.g., the GSO or the IMF), so the risk of data inconsistency in this
context is minimal.

Like most structural models, the study uses historical coefficients to simulate future outcomes.
However, given the rapid structural changes in Viet Nam's economy and potential revisions in
GDP calculations by the GSO, the model's results could be affected. Additionally, structural
models often assume that economic relationships, such as between consumption, investment,
prices, and output, will remain stable in the long term. This assumption may not hold in the
context of Viet Nam's fast economic transition and external shocks like geopolitical changes,
global trade fluctuations, energy prices, or pandemics. To avoid potential biases from detailed
yearly forecasts amid economic uncertainty, the study adopts a fixed GDP growth scenario for
the entire period to compare policy options. This approach ensures consistency and excludes
the impact of uncontrollable fluctuations in the model.

This study acknowledges several inherent limitations arising from the need to simplify
assumptions to facilitate quantitative modelling. The study assumes that businesses are
capable of implementing the necessary emission reduction measures to meet NDC targets, but
in reality, this process is much more complex. Technology transformation requires significant
investment, advanced techniques, skilled labour, and suitable resources. Small and medium-
sized enterprises, particularly in energy-intensive and high-emission sectors, may face
significant challenges in meeting NDC requirements without financial and technological support
from the government. In the "no-ETS" scenarios, the model presumes full implementation of all
identified mitigation measures by businesses. While this assumption allows for straightforward
estimation of emissions and compliance costs, it risks overstating the feasibility of such
implementation in real-world contexts. Technical constraints, investment delays, and
behavioural inertia--particularly among firms operating with limited institutional capacity or
uncertain return expectations--can significantly hinder the adoption of low-carbon
technologies.

For the ETS scenarios, the model assumes a perfectly competitive and fully functional carbon
market, where allowance prices are determined by the equilibrium between supply and
demand. While this assumption offers analytical consistency, it does not fully capture the
structural and behavioural complexities observed in actual emissions trading systems.
Empirical evidence from existing ETS markets suggests that issues such as limited liquidity,
speculative trading, and regulatory uncertainty can cause significant price distortions and
undermine market efficiency. Moreover, the study does not include a sensitivity analysis for key
variables such as carbon price volatility or uncertainty in mitigation costs. These factors can

significantly affect compliance strategies and alter the relative cost-effectiveness of different
policy designs. Future research should incorporate such analyses to strengthen the robustness
and applicability of model findings under varied market and policy conditions.

The model also assumes a uniform capacity among firms to implement emission reductions
required to meet NDC targets. In practice, such capacity varies widely depending on firm size,
financial resources, technological readiness, and sectoral characteristics. Small and medium-
sized enterprises, especially in energy-intensive industries, may struggle to comply without
targeted support. This variation underscores the importance of accurate emissions monitoring
at the facility level to reflect each firm's actual abatement potential. International experience
highlights that an effective Measurement, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system is essential
for ensuring transparency, fairness, and accountability in ETS implementation.

One of the key challenges in accurately determining market prices lies in the lack of available
information from the supply side of carbon credits. To simplify market simulation, the
Consultant assume that credit supply is always available at varying percentage thresholds (e.g.,
10%, 20%), without accounting for the actual cost of credit generation. Under this assumption,
if the initial allowance price is low and credit prices are set equal to allowance prices, the actual
ability of the market to supply credits at levels such as 10% or 20% will ultimately depend on
the production cost structures of credit-generating sectors. If the allowance price is too low, in
reality, credit suppliers may not participate in the market, as the market price would fall below
their investment or abatement costs. This represents a key limitation of the study in
determining a realistic market price.

IV.4. Next steps

To strengthen the analysis and enhance policy relevance, further research is needed to fully
integrate additional emission sectors, beyond the three key sectors (thermal power, cement
manufacturing, and steel production), into the assessment model. Expanding the scope of
analysis will allow for more accurate estimates of the total domestic carbon credit supply and
demand, while also reflecting the technical characteristics, costs, and behaviours of each
emission sector. The assessment of the current carbon credit supply in Viet Nam, along with the
estimation of average investment costs, remains a critical area for continued discussion and
analysis.

In addition to expanding sectoral coverage, future assignments should build on the existing
foundation of ETS operational design developed in earlier studies. The focus should now shift
toward testing and refining key implementation elements, such as simulation of registry
operations, pilot transactions, and the verification of MRV protocols. It will also be important to
assess the institutional capacity and readiness of relevant agencies to manage compliance,
enforcement, and market facilitation functions. Furthermore, targeted research should explore
financial instruments and incentive mechanisms � including linkages to climate finance and
Article 6 of the Paris Agreement � to enhance private sector participation and mobilise
investment for low-carbon technologies. In parallel, pilot applications of blockchain and other
advanced digital technologies should be explored to improve transparency, traceability, and
efficiency in ETS operations, particularly in areas such as emissions data management, tracking
of credits and allowances, and transaction settlement. These practical next steps will support

the transition from ETS policy design to effective pilot operation and long-term market
development.

3. Macroeconomic Model

a. Macroeconomic model methodology

The model is fundamentally built upon the HERMIN structural econometric model. HERMIN is a
smaller-scale version of the HERMES model (Harmonised European Research for Multinational
Economic and Energy Systems), developed in 1988 by Irish professors John Bradley and John
Fitz Gerald. It was designed to study the development of economies transitioning from lower
levels of development, particularly in cases where data availability is limited. The HERMIN model
has been widely applied in 27 EU countries, as well as in regions such as Poland and Turkey,
and is currently being implemented at the National Center for Socio-Economic Information and
Forecasting (NCIF) under the Ministry of Planning and Investment � MPI (MPI was merged into
MOF of Viet Nam).

Key assumptions:

       The model is built on Keynesian demand theory, incorporating the effects of cost and
           price competitiveness.

       It assumes that Viet Nam is a small and open economy, influenced by external prices
           and taxation.

       Factor demand equations are formulated based on the CES (Constant Elasticity of
           Substitution) cost function.

       Price factors are modelled using the Phillips curve, accounting for how wage changes
           impact production costs across the economy

Model structure development

       The model segments the economy into key economic sectors, allowing for an in-depth

           analysis of sectoral changes and their impact on the overall economy.

     It clearly defines and analyzes the transmission mechanisms linking domestic and

          global economic conditions.

     It reflects the openness of the economy through international trade and its responses

          to domestic and external shocks

     It captures the interplay between tradable and non-tradable sectors, technological

          advancements, and structural shifts across industries.

     It models the wage and price determination mechanisms.
     It represents the functioning and openness of the labor market;
     It incorporates the role of the public sector, evaluating trade-offs between public and

          private sector policies.

                            Appendix Figure 1: Macroeconometric Model structure

The model is built on national accounts as its foundation, focusing on three key components:
production, expenditure, and revenue. Production is divided into sectors such as industry,
market services, agriculture, non-market services, and construction, reflecting the structure of
the economy. Expenditure is used to track spending dynamics within the economy. Revenue is
categorized into public sector and private sector revenue. The model functions as an integrated

system, linking production and expenditure through behavioral equations grounded in
Keynesian economic theory. Additionally, identity equations ensure the constraints and
equilibrium of the economy are maintained.

b. The ETS mechanism affects investment through costs and expectations

The structural econometric model used to assess the impact of the ETS is built upon the HERMIN
framework. This model is a type of structural macro-econometric model developed from the
HERMES system (Harmonised European Research for Multinational Economic and Energy
Systems), which was initiated by the European Economic Commission in the 1980s. HERMIN
was developed in 1988 by two Irish economists, Professor John Bradley and Professor John
FitzGerald, with the aim of studying the development trajectories of relatively less developed
European economies. It is particularly well-suited for small and open economies. The model
applied in this context was developed through a technical cooperation and experience-sharing
program between Ireland's Economic and Social Research Institute (ESRI) and Vietnam's
National Centre for Socio-Economic Information and Forecasting (NCIF) under the Ministry of
Planning and Investment � MPI (MPI has been merged into MOF of Viet Nam) starting in 2012,
and was officially published in 2015 at the Economic and Social Research Institute (ESRI)6.

In the model, the variable i_ets represents the level of investment or costs related to ETS
compliance (such as clean technology investment, carbon credit purchases, etc.). This variable
is directly incorporated into the regression equations for industrial capital growth (EQ_KT) and
the services market (EQ_KM) in the form of log (i_ets) or dependent variables such as c_kt_e
(xpected capital costs).

Specifically, an increase in compliance costs raises the expected capital investment costs
(c_kt_e), causing the capital-to-labor ratio (kt_star) to decrease relative to labor. This is reflected
through the CES cost function, where businesses choose the capital-labor usage ratio to
optimize costs. However, when investing in clean technologies (such as through i_ets) which
helps reduce emissions costs in the long term, the clear carbon price signal can stimulate
investment to reduce future carbon credit purchase costs.

In addition, ETS has a spillover effect on GDP through total investment demand in the model
via:

As investment is a component of aggregate demand (GDP = C + I + G + X - M).

When ETS costs are high but are not accompanied by a clear market signal or lack flexible
mechanisms (such as free allocation or credit offsetting), net investment (iv) tends to decrease,
leading to a reduction in real GDP (gdpv).

In the model, iv = iav + imv + itv + inv + icv, all of which indirectly depend on the ETS effect
through the industry's investment decisions.

Regarding the impact on consumption and consumer prices: Consumer prices (phc) are
determined by the consumption equation as log(phc) = f(log(pot), log(poa), ur, log(pm)), which
includes production input prices and the unemployment rate. When ETS investment increases

Global compliance ETS price

ETSs in developed countries tend to be stable and maintain high price levels, while in developing
countries, prices fluctuate more and remain at lower levels. The price decrease in 2024 may
signal an adjustment in policies and global carbon credit market demand.

The ETS in developed countries saw a strong price increase between 2020 and 2023. Specifically,
the average price rose from 13.53 USD (2020) to a peak of 40.98 USD (2024). This reflects
tightening emission reduction policies in recent years.

In contrast, in developing countries, ETS prices also increased significantly from 4.20 USD (2020)
to 6.972 USD (2024). Price volatility in this group is higher compared to developed countries,
suggesting that ETSs here are still unstable and could be influenced by factors such as
inconsistent policies or a decline in carbon credit demand.

Overall, ETS prices in developed countries are significantly higher than in developing countries.
This reflects differences in climate policy commitments, stricter standards, and emission
allowances regulations.

Appendix 3: Minutes of consultation missions
Within the framework of Technical Assistance, the consultation mission was conducted to
discuss and seek feedback on the impact assessment results and recommendations regarding
governance options for ETS in Viet Nam, with a focus on the pilot phase. This consultation
mission included 2 main activities: (1) Bilateral meetings with line ministries; and (2) The
consultation workshop.
I. Bilateral meeting
The Consultant organised bilateral meetings with DCC, the beneficiary of the Technical
Assistance. Additionally, according to the draft amended Decree No. 06/2022/ND-CP, three
sectors, including cement, iron & steel, and thermal power, are considered to be included in the
ETS pilot phase. Therefore, the Consultant consulted with two relevant ministries overseeing
these sectors, including (i) MOIT, which oversees the iron & steel and thermal power sectors;
and (ii) MOC, which oversees the cement sector.
The objectives of bilateral meetings were to discuss and consult the results of the impact
assessment for governance options for Viet Nam's ETS, with a focus on the pilot phase.

I.1.4. Contents

The Consultant presented to MOIT the core components of the ETS design, including the scope
and coverage, cap-setting approaches, and allowance allocation methods. Based on an in-depth
analysis, nine scenarios were proposed for assessing the potential impacts of the ETS. The
Consultant then presented the results of the impact assessment for the various ETS governance
options.

MOIT provided feedback on the allocation methodology, recommending adjustments to
improve fairness and flexibility. The current approach may create inequities as it does not fully
reflect varying technology adoption rates and growth across companies. Special attention
should be given to enterprises with limited further reduction potential due to previous
investments in mitigation technologies. MOIT encouraged drawing on international best
practices to develop a more adaptable allocation mechanism. In response, the Consultant
explained that the proposed allocation formula already considered sectoral growth to align with
Viet Nam's NDC targets. The Consultant stressed that companies have different emission
reduction capacities, and the formula could be refined based on sector-specific targets. The
consultation workshop on 16 April will include enterprise representatives to gather operational
insights.

MOIT emphasised that ensuring the effective and sustainable operation of the ETS requires
prioritising the establishment of a clear carbon pricing mechanism. The Consultant was
encouraged to support the Government in setting an appropriate initial carbon price as a basis
for market operations, with flexibility for adjustments based on supply and demand. MOIT also
recommended developing multiple carbon pricing scenarios to support policy planning and
assess the impacts of various price levels on industries, businesses, and the broader economy.

Additionally, MOIT proposed that the Consultant review and clarify the cost recognition
mechanism related to carbon credit purchases and GHG mitigation measures. It is important to
determine whether these expenses can be considered legitimate business costs under current
regulations. This clarification would provide a legal basis to incentivise enterprise participation
in the ETS and encourage investment in emission reduction solutions. Regarding pricing and
financial matters, the Consultant highlighted the need for close coordination between MOF and
MAE to ensure policy coherence.

the objectives of the consultation mission and introducing the participants. The Consultant also
presented key findings from the impact assessment of ETS governance options during the pilot

DCC emphasised the importance of clearly articulating the rationale behind conducting an ETS
impact assessment and explaining the different types of carbon markets. The Consultant
responded that these aspects had been addressed in the full report and would be further
elaborated during the upcoming consultation workshop.

Regarding the methodology for determining sector inclusion in the ETS, DCC noted that while
trade and emission intensity are valid criteria, it is essential to also consider Viet Nam's specific
context. In particular, three sectors contribute to over 40% of national emissions and should be
prioritised. The Consultant explained that the proposed methodology already incorporates
emission intensity as the core indicator and uses trade intensity to differentiate between
sectors. Sectors with low trade intensity are proposed for inclusion in the ETS pilot phase,
whereas those with high export intensity may require transitional protection measures.

On data-related issues, DCC expressed concern that the current data collection period (2020�
2022) coincided with the COVID-19 pandemic, which led to reduced production levels and may
not accurately reflect present-day realities. The Consultant clarified that, at the time of the
study, enterprise-level GHG inventory reports were not yet available. As these reports are only
required to be submitted starting from 31 March 2025, the Consultant utilised the best available
data from multiple sources to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the analysis.

Regarding the JETP scenario, although JETP implementation in Viet Nam has not yet commenced
and no projects are currently underway, the Consultant noted that the inclusion of a JETP-based
scenario is a requirement outlined in the Technical Assistance's ToR. Furthermore, this inclusion
reflects international interest and signals Viet Nam's elevated climate ambition, thereby
justifying its consideration in the impact assessment.

In terms of policy implications, given the high level of market volatility and competitiveness, the
Consultant was advised to anticipate potential shifts in both domestic and international policies.
For future deliverables, the Consultant will include more detailed analysis of international credit
trading scenarios under Article 6.

The Consultant began the meeting with a brief overview of the Technical Assistance, outlining
the objectives of the consultation mission and introducing the participants. The Consultant also
presented key findings from the impact assessment of ETS governance options during the pilot

MOC expressed concern about the seemingly minor GDP impact of the ETS � approximately
0.5% � and sought further explanation as to why the impact was so limited. The Consultant
clarified that the proposed plan was intentionally designed to minimise macroeconomic
disruptions. The Consultant explained that the allowance allocation formula adopted by the
team differs from the initial one developed by DCC, as it incorporates sector and facility growth
projections. Furthermore, the Consultant noted that the emission factor currently used is an
average across the sector, whereas international practices often rely on top-performing

facilities (e.g., top 10%) to benchmark allocations. It was further highlighted that, with Viet Nam's
GDP nearing USD 500 billion, the total value of 200 million allowances at the estimated market
price would only account for around 0.1% of GDP. This low allowance-to-GDP ratio aligns with
the limited macroeconomic impact observed.

The discussion also addressed fairness concerns in sectors like cement, where facilities vary
significantly in age and technological advancement. Some stakeholders pointed out that older
facilities have already optimised their emissions reduction efforts, potentially placing them at a
disadvantage under a uniform formula. In response, the Consultant explained that while the
current approach applies a single sector-wide emission factor, international ETSs often adopt
"grandparenting" during early phases to accommodate long-operating facilities. However, the
Consultant noted that as the market matures, benchmarking becomes standard practice. It was
added that based on international experiences, facilities encountering difficulties with their
allocations may submit adjustment proposals to the allocation authority. Conversely, facilities
that have already implemented mitigation measures may benefit from the current formula by
generating surplus allowances. Mr. Robert supported this view, noting that carbon trading
within sectors allows higher-emitting facilities to purchase allowances from more efficient
peers. Mr. Frederic, emphasised that technology diversity within sectors strengthens the carbon
market, enabling businesses with varying abatement costs to engage in trading. He further
mentioned that in countries like China, allowance allocation is initially based on projected
output and later adjusted using actual production data, though such an approach demands
strong data management systems.

Regarding the possibility of adopting facility-specific growth factors instead of sector-wide ones,
the Consultant noted that while this is theoretically feasible, it would be challenging to
implement in practice. The Consultant explained that such an approach depends on the sector's
orientation and its capacity to manage data for individual facilities. It was suggested that any
related concerns or issues be raised through relevant industry associations.

Clarifying the timeline for the sectoral growth target, the Consultant confirmed that the
allocation formula includes projections through to 2030. The Consultant also emphasised that,
at a minimum emission reduction targets should align with Viet Nam's unconditional NDC
targets to ensure policy coherence. Furthermore, facility-level GHG inventories will be essential
for future allowance recalibrations. It was noted that piloting the carbon market will help
accelerate improvements in the national data system, enabling Viet Nam to monitor emission
trends and abatement costs more effectively over time.

MOC sought clarification on how the proposed policy might influence sectoral restructuring. In
response, the Consultant discussed opportunities for green transformation, explaining that
carbon pricing mechanisms can incentivise shifts toward low-emission technologies. It was
noted that growing demand for carbon credits, particularly from emission-intensive sectors like
cement, could help stimulate the development of green industries capable of supplying offsets.

Finally, the Consultant provided several international examples of credit methodology
development. In Canada, although a national carbon market was initially planned, only Quebec
implemented one, which eventually linked with California's ETS after six years of methodology
development. For Viet Nam, the Consultant recommended referencing international standards
such as VCS and GS. Examples include Singapore's policy of accepting credits from recognised

standards for domestic offset obligations, China's use of CDM methodologies for domestic
credit projects, and Thailand's sale of Article 6 credits to Switzerland. The Consultant concluded
that encouraging Viet Nam to develop domestic crediting methodologies would not only to
meet compliance needs but also to enable international credit transfers through Letters of
Authorisation.

II.4. PROCEEDINGS
II.4.1. Opening remarks

Mr. Nguyen Tuan Quang, Deputy Director of DCC, delivered the opening remarks by
highlighting Viet Nam's response to global climate change and reaffirming the country's strong
commitment to its obligations under the Paris Agreement, including the goal of achieving net-
zero GHG emissions by 2050. He emphasised that to meet these ambitious targets, Viet Nam
has opted to implement carbon pricing instruments, with a particular focus on developing an
ETS as a key policy tool. According to the roadmap, the carbon market will undergo a pilot phase
through 2028 and transition to full operation from 2029. This workshop is held under the
framework of Technical Assistance funded by UNOPS through ETP. Its primary objective is to
assess the potential impacts to support the effective design and implementation of Viet Nam's
carbon market. The outcomes will inform legal and institutional improvements in preparation
for the pilot phase. Key topics for discussion include market design options, sectoral coverage,
cap-setting, allowance allocation methods (such as free allocation, auctioning, or hybrid
approaches), the integration of domestic carbon credits for offsets, infrastructure and capacity
readiness, and market oversight mechanisms. A thorough analysis of the socio-economic
impacts of various ETS design options on production costs, competitiveness, technological
innovation, and employment, with the feedback and comments from stakeholders, will ensure
that the final approach is practical, effective, and aligned with Viet Nam's unique economic and
social context.

Mr. John Robert Cotton, Deputy Director of ETP, opened the workshop by expressing his
sincere appreciation to all participants. He highlighted the significance of this assessment as a
critical step in supporting the Government of Viet Nam in implementing the ETS, in alignment
with the Law 72/2020/QH14 on Environmental Protection in Viet Nam, Decree 06/2022/ND-CP
on mitigation of GHG emissions and protection of ozone layer, and the national commitment
to achieve net-zero emissions by 2050. Mr. Cotton emphasised that a well-designed carbon
market will not only enable Viet Nam to reduce GHG emissions in a cost-effective manner but
also enhance the global competitiveness of its industries. He noted that this workshop serves
as an important platform to exchange ideas and gather feedback. The impact assessment will
deliver evidence-based analysis on policy design options and their potential economic, social,
and environmental impacts, providing essential input for developing an effective ETS
framework, including cap-setting, sectoral coverage, allowance allocation, and a robust,
transparent MRV system.

                                                               complementary mechanism to regulate
                                                               emitters. However, the carbon tax has been
                                                               delayed, which is why the ETS is still facing
                                                               several challenges.
                                                               First, there is currently no penalty mechanism in
                                                               place, so most facilities are not motivated to
                                                               reduce their emissions due to the lack of clear
                                                               incentives. Secondly, this also affects price
                                                               discovery, as Indonesia's ETS is currently limited
                                                               to the coal-fired thermal power plant sub-sector.
                                                               Since this sector is controlled by a single state-
                                                               owned enterprise, PLN, the allowances are only
                                                               traded among PLN's subsidiaries, making it
                                                               difficult to establish a fair market price. The
                                                               government is also working to implement the
                                                               ETS in other sectors; however, this process is still
                                                               ongoing.
